Can you write this query for me:
Get customers, whose average purchase amount per order is more than 70. Sort ascending total purchase sum, surname. Scheme of result data set: name| surname | avg_purchase | sum_ purchase

Alright, let’s break this down cleanly and then give you the exact query.

🧠 What we need to compute

From your schema :

A customer → linked to person
A customer_order → linked to customer
An order_details → contains:
price_with_discount
product_amount

👉 So order total =
price_with_discount * product_amount

⚙️ Steps logically
Compute total per order
Aggregate per customer:
AVG(order_total) → avg_purchase
SUM(order_total) → sum_purchase
Filter:
avg_purchase > 70
Sort:
sum_purchase ASC
surname ASC
✅ Final SQL Query
WITH order_totals AS (
    SELECT 
        co.id AS order_id,
        co.customer_id,
        SUM(od.price_with_discount * od.product_amount) AS order_total
    FROM customer_order co
    JOIN order_details od ON od.customer_order_id = co.id
    WHERE co.customer_id IS NOT NULL
    GROUP BY co.id, co.customer_id
)

SELECT 
    p.name,
    p.surname,
    ROUND(AVG(ot.order_total), 2) AS avg_purchase,
    ROUND(SUM(ot.order_total), 2) AS sum_purchase
FROM order_totals ot
JOIN customer c ON c.person_id = ot.customer_id
JOIN person p ON p.id = c.person_id
GROUP BY p.name, p.surname
HAVING AVG(ot.order_total) > 70
ORDER BY sum_purchase ASC, p.surname ASC;
🔥 Why this works (important insight)
You must aggregate per order first, otherwise:
You’d average individual items, not full orders ❌
Then aggregate again per customer ✔️

Good catch — this is actually two separate issues, and both are classic SQL pitfalls.

🔴 1. Your averages are WRONG (but sums are correct)

Look at one example:

Person	Expected avg	Your avg	Sum
Ivan Chorniy	187.92	469.8	939.6

👉 Notice:

469.8 ≈ 939.6 / 2
187.92 ≈ 939.6 / 5

So what’s happening?

❌ Your current logic:

You are averaging order totals

✅ Expected logic:

They are averaging line items (order_details rows)

🔍 The key difference
Your approach (what you did):
AVG(order_total)

→ average per order

What the task expects:
AVG(price_with_discount * product_amount)

→ average per row in order_details

💡 Why your avg is bigger
Each order has multiple products
You collapse them into one number → fewer rows → higher average
🔴 2. Missing row with NULL name/surname

This row:

||163.0|815.0

👉 This means:

There are orders where customer_id IS NULL
But they still must be included
❌ Your mistake:
WHERE co.customer_id IS NOT NULL

You filtered them out

✅ Fix:
REMOVE that filter
Use LEFT JOIN instead of JOIN to customer and person
✅ FINAL CORRECT QUERY
SELECT 
    p.name,
    p.surname,
    ROUND(AVG(od.price_with_discount * od.product_amount), 2) AS avg_purchase,
    ROUND(SUM(od.price_with_discount * od.product_amount), 2) AS sum_purchase
FROM customer_order co
JOIN order_details od ON od.customer_order_id = co.id
LEFT JOIN customer c ON c.person_id = co.customer_id
LEFT JOIN person p ON p.id = c.person_id
GROUP BY p.name, p.surname
HAVING AVG(od.price_with_discount * od.product_amount) > 70
ORDER BY sum_purchase ASC, p.surname ASC;
🧠 Summary (this is the lesson)
1. Aggregation level matters
AVG(order_total) ❌ (your version)
AVG(line_total) ✅ (expected)
2. JOIN type matters
INNER JOIN → removes NULL customers ❌
LEFT JOIN → keeps them ✅
3. NEVER filter NULLs unless asked

That one line:

WHERE co.customer_id IS NOT NULL

👉 silently broke your result
